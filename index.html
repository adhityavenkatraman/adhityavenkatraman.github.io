Finally, I wrote my senior honors thesis on the effect individual representatives have on the federal funds allocated to their own district. This project builds on research conducted this past summer with Professor Cameron Shelton. Drawing on Shigeo Hiranoâ€™s 2011 article on the Japanese Diet, I am exploring these effects in the context of the United States. Thus far, the project has involved collecting, cleaning, and transforming massive datasets about federal spending at the district level. Projects

My Github features a few of my recent projects, including an implementation of Google's foundational Pagerank algorithm, an automated API performance and scale testing tool, and a pair of projects that leverage data science to explore political ideology in media and campaign finance. The first of these examines political bias in social media using natural language processing. I published an article in Toward Data Science, which you can read here! We looked at over 140,000 Congressional tweets to understand how officials disseminate information in the era of fake news. In the second project, I examine data on the flow of donations in presidential elections from 1980-2016 in relation to the ideology of donors and recipients. You can read the report in either html or pdf, while the repository with all the code and figures is here. I make a number of interesting findings. First, the most prolific donors seem to have more extreme viewpoints and ideologies. Second, there seems to be a negative relationship between the size of contributions and the number of contributions made. This relationship seems to decrease logarithmically. Finally, I find that elite, powerful donors donate more than non-elites on average. I also find that the distribution of political ideology may be less extreme among elites than it is in the general population. I conclude by summarizing my findings, acknowledging major limitations, and offering recommendations for future work.
